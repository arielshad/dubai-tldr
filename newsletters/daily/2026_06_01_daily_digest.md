<h2 style="margin: 0 0 8px; font-size: 24px; font-weight: 800; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.2;">UAE's New Civil Code Takes Effect Today — Dubai SPAs Get a 12-Month Defect Window</h2>

<p style="margin: 0 0 20px; font-size: 15px; color: #8a8a85; line-height: 1.5;">The 1985 Civil Code retires on 1 June 2026 — Dubai SPAs, usufruct deals and broker disclosure standards all shift today.</p>

<p style="margin: 0 0 16px; font-size: 15px; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.6;">
<strong style="color: #f7ff00;">What is it?</strong><br>
Federal Decree-Law No. 25 of 2025 — the first full rewrite of the UAE Civil Code in 40 years — switches on today, 1 June 2026. TradeArabia and Gulf News confirmed the property-relevant changes Dubai brokers and buyers need to price in immediately.
</p>

<p style="margin: 0 0 16px; font-size: 15px; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.6;">
<strong style="color: #f7ff00;">How does it work?</strong><br>
Three reforms matter most: latent-defect liability doubles to 12 months from delivery; usufructuary-rights contracts (long leases, free-zone instruments) must be registered or are void; and a new statutory pre-contract disclosure duty makes withholding material facts a basis for civil claims.
</p>

<p style="margin: 0 0 16px; font-size: 15px; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.6;">
<strong style="color: #f7ff00;">Why does it matter?</strong><br>
Every Dubai SPA template, RERA Form A/B/F and lease drafted after today needs a fresh legal review — silent contracts now default to the new code. Brokers face a documented disclosure duty, and operators with usufructuary positions in DIFC or ADGM must confirm registration or risk nullity.
</p>

<p style="margin: 0 0 20px; font-size: 15px; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.6;">
<strong style="color: #f7ff00;">Who is it for?</strong><br>
Dubai conveyancers and SPA drafters, broker compliance leads, free-zone operators on usufructuary positions, off-plan buyers receiving 2026 handovers, and lenders sizing post-handover warranty risk.
</p>

<p style="margin: 0 0 20px; font-size: 15px; color: #8a8a85; line-height: 1.5;">Indian buyers held 20.59% of Dubai property purchases through the March geopolitical shock — the cleanest post-crisis nationality scoreboard since the Hormuz event.</p>

<p style="margin: 0 0 16px; font-size: 15px; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.6;">
<strong style="color: #f7ff00;">What is it?</strong><br>
Gulf Today's 29 May piece compiles DXB Interact transaction data to score the top 17 buyer nationalities since end-Feb 2026 — the first Dubai-wide read of who actually wrote cheques during the 90-day shock window, not just who applied for visas.
</p>

<p style="margin: 0 0 16px; font-size: 15px; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.6;">
<strong style="color: #f7ff00;">How does it work?</strong><br>
Indians wrote 20.59% of purchases, with the British (13.26%), Egyptians (12.60%), Americans (8.99%) and Pakistanis (6.94%) rounding out the top five — collectively 62.38% of all post-crisis flow. As a regional bloc, Asians lead at 35.36%, Europeans 30.68%, Africans 16.82%, North Americans 12.04%.
