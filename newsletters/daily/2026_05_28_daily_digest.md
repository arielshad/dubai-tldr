<p style="margin: 0 0 16px; font-size: 15px; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.6;">
<strong style="color: #f7ff00;">What is it?</strong><br>
Khaleej Times (citing eToro analyst Nagham Hassan) reports Dubai's 2025 investor pool hit 193,000+ active participants, with resident buyers contributing over half of investment value, and the average UAE renter taking just 4.8 years to become a homeowner.
</p>

<p style="margin: 0 0 16px; font-size: 15px; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.6;">
<strong style="color: #f7ff00;">How does it work?</strong><br>
The shift ties directly to the Dubai Land Department's First Time Home Buyer Programme — credited with helping 2,000+ residents purchase by January 2026. Q1 2026 tape hit AED 252B (+31% YoY) on the back of a record AED 917B in 2025.
</p>

<p style="margin: 0 0 16px; font-size: 15px; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.6;">
<strong style="color: #f7ff00;">Why does it matter?</strong><br>
If the structural buyer has shifted from non-resident flipper to resident long-hold, it changes how brokers pitch (use vs yield), how developers price post-handover plans, and how lenders price LTV — primary-residence dynamics layered on top of the existing investor base.

<h2 style="margin: 0 0 8px; font-size: 24px; font-weight: 800; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.2;">Dubai Real Estate Court cancels a Business Bay hotel-unit sale and reverses the buyer's registration after a four-year, AED 443K installment default — developer keeps the unit, buyer pays costs</h2>

<p style="margin: 0 0 20px; font-size: 15px; color: #8a8a85; line-height: 1.5;">Stop paying after handover and Dubai's Real Estate Court will reverse your title: a fresh precedent on what post-handover default actually costs.</p>

<p style="margin: 0 0 16px; font-size: 15px; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.6;">
<strong style="color: #f7ff00;">What is it?</strong><br>
The Dubai Real Estate Court (24-May-2026) terminated a Business Bay hotel-apartment sale worth AED 443,000, cancelled the buyer's title, re-registered the unit to the developer, and ordered the buyer to pay all fees. The property was ready since 2021 but installments stopped in July 2025.
</p>

<p style="margin: 0 0 16px; font-size: 15px; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.6;">
<strong style="color: #f7ff00;">How does it work?</strong><br>
Under Law 13 of 2008, a developer can terminate the contract and reverse title registration even after handover when payments are missed — the defaulting buyer absorbs all administrative, registration and legal costs on top of losing any equity paid.
</p>

<p style="margin: 0 0 16px; font-size: 15px; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.6;">
<strong style="color: #f7ff00;">Why does it matter?</strong><br>
Post-handover payment plans are now the default selling tool for Dubai developers, but this ruling confirms that "post-handover" does not mean "cannot be reversed." Brokers pitching default-resilience to buyers need to price the legal optionality the developer retains.
</p>

<p style="margin: 0 0 20px; font-size: 15px; color: #f5f5f0; line-height: 1.6;">
<strong style="color: #f7ff00;">Who is it for?</strong><br>
Off-plan buyers running tight cash plans; brokers selling post-handover structures; conveyancers advising defaulting buyers on restructure options; distressed-asset desks tracking developer-recovered inventory.
</p>
